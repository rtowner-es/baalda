# OpenContext Trademark Policy

OpenContext's **source code** is free and open under the [Apache License
2.0](LICENSE). This trademark policy is separate from that license: it
governs the **name and brand**, not the code.

The Apache-2.0 license grants you broad rights to use, modify, and
redistribute the software. It does **not** grant any rights to use the
OpenContext name or brand (see Section 6 of the license). This document
explains what is and isn't allowed, so you can build on OpenContext with
confidence while the project keeps a clear identity.

## Marks covered

- The names **"OpenContext"** and **"Context"** (as used to identify this
  project and product).
- The OpenContext logo(s) and wordmark.
- Confusingly similar variations of the above.

## You may, without asking

- **Use the software freely** under the Apache-2.0 license, including for
  commercial purposes and to run your own hosted service.
- **State compatibility or origin truthfully**, e.g. "built with
  OpenContext", "powered by OpenContext", "a fork of OpenContext", or
  "compatible with OpenContext" — as long as it doesn't imply endorsement
  or that your project *is* the official OpenContext.
- **Refer to the project by name** in articles, tutorials, talks, and
  documentation.
- **Keep the marks intact** in unmodified redistributions of the software.

## You may not, without written permission

- **Name your fork, product, service, or company "OpenContext"** (or a
  confusingly similar name). If you distribute a modified version, please
  give it a distinct name and make clear it is not the official project.
- **Offer a hosted or commercial service under the OpenContext name** or
  brand as if it were the official one.
- **Use the OpenContext logo or wordmark as your own** product/company logo.
- **Register** the marks (or confusingly similar ones), or any related
  domain names, social handles, or app-store listings, as trademarks.
- **Imply sponsorship, affiliation, or endorsement** by the OpenContext
  project where none exists.

## Why this matters

The code is meant to be copied, self-hosted, and improved — that's the
point of open source. The name exists so users can tell the official
project (and any official hosted service) apart from forks and third-party
offerings. Protecting the name protects users from confusion; it does not
restrict your right to the code.
